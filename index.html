Introduction

One of Scotland’s greatest natural treasures is its extensive areas of land with minimal light pollution, where we can still observe truly dark skies. Importantly, such areas can be accessed with some ease. Scottish dark skies have their well-known and expansive counterpart above what is now Canada. It is therefore natural for the University of Aberdeen and the University of Calgary — alongside other partners — to come together in the exploration of this phenomenon.

The workshop will explore this theme from a multidisciplinary perspective. In the context of the humanities, we will ask how the experience of being exposed to skies unpolluted by artificial light shaped the development of human culture, religion, and science in antiquity. Modern sciences will bring other pertinent questions, such as what the term ‘dark sky’ actually means in terms of physics, or how light pollution affects fauna, flora, and humans. Further, legal and ethical aspects of light pollution will be discussed in the context of policymaking. In exploring these questions, we will gain insights also from observational practices grounded in community knowledge.

This exploratory workshop will take place on the campus of the University of Aberdeen in Old Aberdeen but with hybrid delivery of lectures and presentations, welcoming participants and speakers in person and online alike. The event is open to professional academics, students at all levels, pupils of secondary schools, members of local non-govermental organisations, and other members of the public. It is envisaged as the first in a series of events dedicated to dark skies, in the past and today.

Keynote Lecture 1: Dark Skies in the Past
8:30/15:30/18:30
history of science
Mapping Time and Space – The First Ancient Astronomers
by Professor Eshbal Ratzon, Tel Aviv University, Israel (in person)
Show / hide summary The first ancient astronomers did not just describe the movement of stars; they had to invent a whole new language to do so. This lecture explores how ancient Near Eastern cultures, especially Egypt and Babylon, created tools to map the sky, build time units, and use mathematics to explain what they saw. Instead of a simple shift from myth to science, we will see how these cultures combined both approaches to develop their knowledge. Moving to the Greco-Roman world, the lecture will show how Greek thinkers introduced new questions, blending philosophical debate with astronomical observation and looking at the universe from an outside perspective rather than from Earth. We will examine why the heliocentric theory—which already existed in antiquity—was rejected at the time. Finally, we will discuss how the Hellenistic period bridged the gap between Babylonian empirical data and Greek logical models, culminating in Ptolemy’s Almagest and his complex system of epicycles and eccentric circles that became the standard astronomical theory for over a millennium.

Sharing Community Knowledge 1

islamic traditions
Dark Skies as a Night Clock – Prayer Times in Palestine before Electrification
by Ahmad Hroub, University of Aberdeen External Research Collaborator, Palestine (online)
Show / hide summary It is well known that Muslims are meant to pray five times a day. Today you will see many Muslims using smartphone applications that display prayer times to know when to pray. Also in mosques it is now common to see sets of electronic clocks indicating the time of each prayer in a given day. But how did Muslims know when to pray before the advent of such devices? Great achievements of pre-modern Islamic astronomy in such centres of learning like Baghdad notwithstanding, local communities had developed practical and yet precise ways of determining the time of prayer. In this context, in my talk you will hear about Palestine before the introduction of electricity during the British Mandate and in the early years of electrification – as remembered by the generation of my grand-grand-parents and grand-parents. As much as the sun guided them in day prayers, it was dark skies that served as their clock for dawn and night prayers.

astronomy
Did you Know the Stars are Disappearing?
– What the world is losing as a result of the overuse of light at night
by Dr Philip Langill, University of Calgary, Canada (online)
Show / hide summary The Rothney Astrophysical Observatory (RAO) has a very busy community engagement team and one day I was invited to spend some time with youngsters in Grade 4 who were at the RAO learning about sky science. I happened to be on hand that day and the RAO team thought the school group would benefit from a spontaneous discussion about light pollution. My opening line was, ‘Did you know the stars were disappearing?’. Their hands shot up with offers as to the reason why – black holes had to be the culprit! Their young minds were as far away from what I was trying to get them to think about as could be. My initial reaction was amusement but that quickly subsided. In my extensive travels down the road of light pollution education I have discovered that it is not just grade 4’ers that are oblivious to its perils, it is the vast majority of everybody. The loss of natural darkness at night is a sudden global phenomenon with many downsides. In this presentation I will share some ideas and perspectives that I hope will spark your curiosity and interest.

Session 1: Undarkened Skies – Impact Below & Above

biology
The Need for Darkness – The effects of light pollution on nature
by Dr Martin Barker, University of Aberdeen (in person)
Show / hide summary Nature has evolved over millions of years to respond to predictable, dependable patterns of darkness. Yet, in a relatively short time, artificial light at night (ALAN) has come to dominate areas that humans share with nature. Light pollution affects about 23% of the world's land area (and is still increasing ~2.2% annually) and not just in urban centres. The loss of darkness has been shown to have a profound, disruptive effect on species that have been studied so far. We briefly summarise some of the work that has been done on animals (insects, birds, mammals, amphibians and reptiles) and plants (crops, trees). But there are still many knowledge gaps in what is becoming an important research area in biology.

scottish history
Observations by Moonlight: The Midnight Excursions of William MacGillivray
by William Vasiladiotis, PhD Candidate in History, University of Aberdeen (in person)
Show / hide summary It was the opinion of the Encyclopaedia Brittanica’s ninth edition that William MacGillivray (1796-1852) was ‘the greatest and most original ornithological genius save one (who did not live long enough to make his powers widely known) that this island [Great Britain] has produced.’ In 1819, he walked the entire distance between Aberdeen and London and made detailed observations of the flora and fauna along the way. While this was an exceptionally arduous journey, MacGillivray’s methods of collecting scientific specimens often involved long excursions regardless of the weather that were chronicled in various journals. This paper will examine his travels by night, often out of necessity, and the ways in which ‘the cloudless sky, spangled with stars’ both aided and hindered his scientific observations in the first half of the nineteenth century.

Session 3: Capturing Dark Skies in Literature & Art
7:30/14:30/17:30
ancient jewish literature
Night, Dawn, and Deliverance – Darkness Imagery in the David Traditions of the Books of Samuel and the Psalms
by Michael Weinburg, Independent Scholar (PhD Bar-Ilan University), Arad, Israel (online)
Show / hide summary Many Ancient Near Eastern and Greco-Roman traditions portray night/darkness and dawn/light as opposing cosmic realities associated with competing divine beings or forces of chaos and order. By contrast, the Hebrew Bible presents both night and day as creations of the God of Israel, who exercises sovereign authority over both realms. Although biblical literature preserves many traditional associations of darkness with danger and uncertainty and of dawn with restoration and deliverance, it reinterprets these motifs within a fundamentally monotheistic framework. This paper examines how the David traditions preserved in the books of Samuel provide the interpretive framework for understanding the imagery of night and dawn in the Psalms. Focusing on Psalm 3 as a test case and its superscriptional connection to David's flight from Absalom (2 Samuel 15–19), the study argues that the night functions not merely as a period of danger but as the divinely appointed setting of God's preservation, guidance, and deliverance, while the dawn serves as the visible realization of his justice and vindication of David, his anointed king. Rather than representing competing cosmic realms, night and dawn become successive stages in God's sovereign work of preserving and vindicating his chosen king.
8:00/15:00/18:00
cultural history
Between Heaven and Earth – The Aesthetics of the Night Sky in Chinese Art
by Dr Phillip Grimberg, University of Heidelberg & Friedrich-Alexander University Erlangen-Nuremberg, Germany (in person)
Show / hide summary This paper explores the role of the night sky in Chinese art from the Northern Song dynasty (960-1127) to the present. It argues that Chinese artists rarely treated the heavens as an astronomical spectacle. Instead, they created aesthetic experiences of night through emptiness, atmosphere, moonlight, and spatial ambiguity. Drawing on Chinese cosmology, Daoist philosophy, landscape painting, poetry, and contemporary art, the paper examines how the heavens were often evoked rather than directly depicted. Through a number of case studies, it demonstrates that the night sky functioned as a space of contemplation, memory, and cosmic reflection. The paper proposes that the history of the night sky in Chinese art is ultimately a history of perception and humanity’s relationship to the cosmos.

Session 5: Sciences & the Humanities in the Service of Dark Skies
10:30/17:30/20:30
astronomy
Getting Around Light Pollution? Can we use modern technology to reclaim the stars?
by Dr Philip Langill, University of Calgary, Canada (online)
Show / hide summary Astronomers are scientists that attempt to answer the most basic questions one can ask about the cosmos.  And like all scientists, those questions are tackled by making delicate measurements.  But what do they measure, you ask?  Fundamentally, astronomers measure very carefully some aspect of the light that comes from those wonderful and very distant objects they are trying to study.  The difficulty is that the tools used to scoop up that distant light also scoops light from every other nearby source including human-made.  In this talk I will discuss some of these details and describe what astronomers can and cannot do about the background light that makes delicate measurements difficult.  For those with a casual interest, I will touch on some tools that are available for astrophotographers.

Session 1: Brightened Skies – the Sun & the Aurora
2:00/9:00/12:00
history of science
Where is the Sun at night? Heavenly Abode of Luminaries in Ancient Near Eastern Literature
by Professor Eshbal Ratzon, Tel Aviv University, Israel (in person)
Show / hide summary This paper will explore ancient Jewish conceptions of the whereabouts of celestial luminaries—the sun, moon, and stars—when they are not visible. Drawing on sources from the Hebrew Bible, Second Temple literature, and comparative ancient Near Eastern traditions, we will examine the widespread notion that these luminaries reside in heavenly abodes when hidden from human sight. While ancient Near Eastern cultures often imagined the sun traversing the underworld at night, the Hebrew Bible consistently situates the sun and stars in the heavens even when they are not visible. This view is further developed in Second Temple literature, especially the Qumran scrolls and the Book of Enoch, where the luminaries are depicted not only as heavenly beings but often as angelic entities performing divine functions. I will argue that these mythological perceptions persisted and intensified in apocalyptic writings of the Second Temple period, despite biblical efforts to downplay celestial worship.

Session 3: Modern Insights into/from Dark Skies
8:00/15:00/18:00
medicine
Out of Sync – How Modern Life Disrupts Your Body Clock
by Dr Brendan Gabriel, University of Aberdeen (in person)
Show / hide summary Life on Earth evolved under a predictable cycle of light and dark. Every cell in our bodies contains a molecular clock, and when that clock falls out of step with the environment, there are consequences. The modern 24-hour society, with its shift work, artificial lighting, and social jetlag, drives precisely this kind of misalignment. This talk explores how chronic circadian disruption is linked to increased risk of metabolic disease, particularly type 2 diabetes, and how it affects skeletal muscle, the tissue most responsible for regulating blood glucose. Drawing on recent research, the case will be made that desynchrony from natural light-dark cycles is an underappreciated driver of cardiometabolic ill health.

Show / hide summary Can a planetarium give us a real dark skies experience? We will try to imagine the Milky Way and constellations under dark sky conditions.
